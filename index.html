Strategy Mindset

How I think about problems, industries, and ideas. These are topics I find myself coming back to ‚Äî written in my own words, not polished for an audience.

Operational efficiency is a strategic asset, not just a cost play

Operations

Most people treat process improvement as a back-office function ‚Äî something you do to cut costs. But at BMO, I saw it differently. When we automated the overdraft fee process and freed up 45 hours a week, the real gain wasn't the time saved ‚Äî it was the capacity that unlocked. Teams could take on higher-value work, relationships improved, and the org became more adaptive. That's a strategic outcome. Efficiency done right creates optionality, and optionality is what good strategy is built on.

NPS scores tell you what happened. They don't tell you why.

Customer Strategy

When I was analyzing 10,000+ client NPS responses at Legacy Bank, I noticed something: the score itself was almost a distraction. The real signal was in the qualitative comments ‚Äî the patterns in why clients were frustrated. A score of 43 doesn't tell you what to fix. But when you cluster the verbatims and map them against client segments, you start to see the actual failure points. That's what informed the policy changes that moved us to 92. Data tells you what; qualitative listening tells you why. You need both.

The most underrated part of business strategy: never losing sight of the main goal

Business Strategy

In my experience, the biggest execution risk in any strategy isn't a bad plan ‚Äî it's drift. Teams get deep into their workstreams, KPIs multiply, and somewhere along the way the original objective becomes background noise. I've seen it happen in banking, where process improvements became ends in themselves rather than means to better client outcomes. The strategies that actually land are the ones where leadership keeps the main goal visible and every team ‚Äî from operations to client delivery to finance ‚Äî can draw a straight line from their day-to-day work back to that goal. Alignment isn't a kickoff meeting. It's an ongoing discipline. The best strategists I've worked with treated cross-functional alignment as part of the strategy itself, not a soft skill on the side.

Sustainability isn't an ESG checkbox ‚Äî it's a value chain strategy

Sustainability

Most companies approach sustainability the wrong way ‚Äî they set emissions targets, publish a report, and call it done. But real sustainability isn't about hitting benchmarks. It's about rethinking the entire value chain: how you source, how you produce, how you deliver, and how you adapt over time. The companies that will win long-term aren't the ones with the best ESG score today ‚Äî they're the ones flexible and forward-thinking enough to build sustainability into how they actually operate and compete.

A framework my professor shared has stuck with me: Resources Build Capabilities ‚Üí Capabilities Build Competencies ‚Üí Competencies enable a Competitive Advantage. Applied to sustainability, this means investing in the right resources now ‚Äî cleaner processes, circular supply chains, adaptive talent ‚Äî builds capabilities that compound into genuine competencies. And those competencies become a competitive moat that's nearly impossible to replicate. Sustainability, done right, isn't a cost center. It's a long-term growth strategy.

Business Monitoring and Sales Enablement: two sides of the same coin

Business Monitoring

People often treat business monitoring as a reporting function ‚Äî dashboards, KPIs, variance analysis. And sales enablement as a content and training function ‚Äî decks, playbooks, onboarding. But when you step back, they're both solving the same problem: making it easier for the sales team to do their job well.

Business monitoring tells you where friction exists ‚Äî which accounts are stalling, which processes are creating delays, which metrics are trending the wrong way. Sales enablement uses that intelligence to remove the friction ‚Äî better tools, clearer messaging, faster access to the right information at the right time. In its simplest form, the goal of both is to get out of the sales team's way and give them everything they need to close. The organizations that connect these two functions ‚Äî rather than siloing them ‚Äî are the ones where sales actually scales.
